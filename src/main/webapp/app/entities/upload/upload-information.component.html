Introduction

Nucleobase transporters are members of the APC superfamily of secondary active transporters (Saier et al, 2016) and are very important for the cells since their substrate uptake may function as nitrogen/carbon source, affect energy production, signaling, replication, transcription and protein synthesis through the salvage pathways for nucleotide synthesis (Papakostas et al, 2013; King et al, 2006). In addition to their important direct role on the central metabolism of the cell, they have been regarded as potentially important drug targets for viral/parasitic diseases, for cancer (Köse & Schiedel, 2009) or even mammalian physiology (sleep, drug and alcohol abuse, pain), since they regulate the levels of free nucleosides in circulation .

There exist 4 known families of transporters that use nucleobases as their main substrates, the Nucleobase-Cation Symporter (NCS) families NCS1 (absent in Metazoa) and NCS2 (or NAT/NCS2) (universally present) that belong to the APC superfamily, and two minor families that are found exclusively in Plants and belong to the distinct DMT superfamily. The NAT/NCS2 family distinguishes from the others because it is very well conserved and wide-spread across many different taxa (Frillingos, 2012).

Based on an evolutionary and bioinformatics analysis pipeline that we have already implemented in another superfamily, the tRNA synthetases – see (Chaliotis et al. 2017), we have generated a hierarchical evolutionary classification of the NAT/NCS2 family for prokaryotes. This classification helps to assign family members to evolutionary groups with distinct substrate preferences and identify key conserved and functional amino acids that affect substrate specificity.

Towards this end, we have generated Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) and conserved MEME motifs for each level and evolutionary group. These HMMs and MEME motifs are used in our webserver to scan, identify and classify NAT/NCS2 proteins. In addition, structural and functional information from mutational studies is integrated to easily identify key amino acids and assess their level of conservation.
